Full Arch Dental Implants

When multiple teeth have failed or dentures are no longer viable, full arch treatment provides a fixed, stable foundation. This is where planning matters most—and where outcomes depend on getting the surgical and prosthetic details right.

Considering full arch treatment? Request Assessment

What Are Full Arch Dental Implants?

Full arch implants replace an entire upper or lower arch of teeth using strategically placed implants—typically four to six—to support a fixed prosthetic bridge. The technique is often referred to as "All-on-4" or "All-on-X" depending on the number of implants used.

Unlike removable dentures that rest on soft tissue and depend on adhesives for retention, full arch restorations are permanently fixed to the implants. They're designed to function like natural teeth, allowing you to eat, speak, and maintain oral hygiene without the limitations that come with removable prosthetics.

Clinical Advantages of Full Arch Treatment

Full arch implant rehabilitation offers functional and biological advantages over conventional dentures and other tooth replacement approaches:

✓ Fixed Stability

Restorations remain stable during function. No movement during eating or speaking, no reliance on adhesive products.

✓ Dietary Freedom

Return to foods that are difficult or impossible to manage with dentures. Normal masticatory function is restored in most cases.

✓ Prosthetic Aesthetics

Restorations are designed to suit facial proportions and soft tissue architecture. Appearance is individualised, not standardised.

✓ Bone Preservation

Implants provide mechanical loading to bone, reducing the progressive resorption seen with conventional dentures in many patients.

✓ Functional Predictability

When properly planned and executed, full arch restorations provide stable, long-term function with appropriate maintenance.

✓ Psychological Impact

Many patients report improved confidence and reduced self-consciousness compared to their experience with removable prosthetics.

How the All-on-4/All-on-X Protocol Works

The clinical rationale behind full arch implant placement involves several key principles:

  1. Strategic Positioning: Implants are placed in areas of the jaw with optimal bone density and volume, often avoiding the need for extensive grafting procedures.
  2. Angulation Considerations: Posterior implants may be angled to engage available bone and achieve adequate anteroposterior spread for prosthetic support.
  3. Immediate Loading Protocols: In suitable cases, a provisional fixed restoration can be provided on the day of surgery, though this depends on achieving adequate primary stability.
  4. Biomechanical Distribution: Implant positions are planned to distribute occlusal forces evenly across the arch, supporting long-term prosthetic stability.

This approach allows for predictable outcomes in many cases while potentially reducing treatment complexity compared to traditional multiple-implant protocols. However, not every patient is a candidate for immediate loading, and treatment plans must be individualised based on clinical findings.

Candidacy for Full Arch Implants

Full arch treatment is appropriate for some patients but not all. Suitability depends on several clinical and patient-related factors:

  • Most or all teeth in an arch are missing or have been deemed non-restorable
  • Remaining teeth are failing despite attempts at conventional treatment
  • Current dentures are non-functional due to bone resorption or patient intolerance
  • Adequate bone volume exists, or you're willing to consider grafting procedures where indicated
  • General health status allows for surgical intervention and healing
  • Non-smoker, or willingness to cease smoking before and after treatment
  • Realistic expectations regarding outcomes, maintenance requirements, and prosthetic lifespan

During your assessment, we'll evaluate oral health, bone anatomy via CBCT imaging, medical history, and treatment objectives to determine whether full arch treatment is clinically appropriate. Where additional procedures such as bone grafting are required, these will be discussed with realistic timelines and outcome expectations.

The Treatment Pathway

1. Initial Assessment (60-90 minutes)

Comprehensive evaluation includes:

  • Discussion of your concerns, dental history, and treatment goals
  • CBCT scan to assess bone quality, volume, and anatomical considerations
  • Digital photographs and diagnostic records
  • Treatment planning discussion, including whether you're a candidate for immediate loading
  • Written treatment plan with itemised cost breakdown

2. Diagnostic Planning Phase (1-2 weeks)

Digital planning determines surgical and prosthetic approach:

  • Virtual implant placement based on bone anatomy and prosthetic requirements
  • Fabrication of surgical guide for guided implant placement
  • Design and fabrication of provisional restoration (if immediate loading is planned)
  • Finalisation of treatment timeline and surgical protocol

3. Surgical Phase (3-4 hours)

Implant placement and provisional restoration delivery (where clinically appropriate):

  • Extraction of any remaining non-restorable teeth
  • Implant placement using guided surgery protocols
  • Provisional fixed bridge attachment (if primary stability allows for immediate loading)
  • Post-operative instructions, medications, and follow-up scheduling

4. Osseointegration Period (3-6 months)

During the healing and integration phase:

  • Implants integrate with surrounding bone tissue
  • Provisional restoration remains in place (if immediate loading was used)
  • Regular review appointments to monitor healing and tissue response
  • Dietary restrictions gradually relaxed as healing progresses

5. Definitive Prosthetic Phase (2-3 appointments)

Following confirmed osseointegration:

  • Impressions or digital scans for definitive bridge fabrication
  • Try-in appointments to verify fit, occlusion, and aesthetics
  • Delivery of final restoration with occlusal adjustments
  • Maintenance protocols and hygiene instructions

Recovery Expectations

Post-Surgical Recovery

Most patients experience:

  • Discomfort: Typically manageable with prescribed or over-the-counter analgesia. Pain levels vary considerably between patients.
  • Oedema: Swelling usually peaks around day three and subsides over the following week.
  • Dietary modification: Soft diet required for 4-6 weeks to protect implants during early healing.
  • Return to normal activities: Most patients resume work within 2-3 days, though this depends on the extent of surgery and individual healing response.

Long-Term Maintenance Requirements

Implant longevity depends on ongoing maintenance:

  • Daily oral hygiene using techniques specific to full arch restorations
  • Use of interdental cleaning aids and irrigation devices to clean prosthetic-tissue interfaces
  • Professional maintenance appointments every six months
  • Specialist hygiene cleaning by clinicians trained in implant maintenance
  • Smoking cessation, as tobacco use significantly increases risk of complications

Longevity and Prosthetic Lifespan

With appropriate maintenance, dental implants can remain functional for 20+ years in many cases. The prosthetic bridge typically requires replacement every 10-15 years due to wear, though the implants themselves usually remain in situ.

Factors influencing long-term outcomes include:

  • Quality of oral hygiene and patient compliance with maintenance protocols
  • Regular professional maintenance and early intervention when issues arise
  • Smoking status—tobacco use is associated with increased implant failure rates
  • Systemic health conditions affecting healing and bone metabolism
  • Quality of initial surgical placement and prosthetic design

Common Questions About Full Arch Treatment

What level of discomfort should I expect?

Surgery is performed under local anaesthesia, so you won't feel pain during the procedure itself. Post-operative discomfort varies significantly between patients but is generally manageable with prescribed analgesia. Many patients report less discomfort than anticipated, though this isn't universal.

Will I have teeth during the treatment process?

In most cases, yes. If you're a candidate for immediate loading, a provisional fixed restoration is provided on the day of surgery. However, immediate loading isn't suitable for all patients—some cases require a delayed loading protocol with a removable temporary prosthesis during healing.

Can both arches be treated simultaneously?

While technically possible, we typically recommend treating one arch at a time. This approach makes post-operative recovery more manageable and ensures you can eat comfortably throughout treatment. Individual circumstances may vary.

What if bone volume is inadequate?

The All-on-4/All-on-X protocol is designed to utilise available bone efficiently, often avoiding extensive grafting. However, some cases do require bone augmentation or alternative approaches such as zygomatic implants. Assessment and CBCT imaging clarify what's needed in your specific situation.

When can I return to normal eating?

You'll need to follow a soft diet for 4-6 weeks while implants integrate. After this initial healing phase, dietary restrictions are gradually relaxed. Once osseointegration is confirmed and the definitive restoration is in place, most patients can eat normally, though extremely hard or sticky foods may still pose risks to prosthetic components.

What are the success rates?

Full arch implants show high survival rates in the literature—generally above 95% at five years when placed by experienced clinicians in appropriate cases. However, success depends on multiple factors including bone quality, surgical technique, prosthetic design, patient compliance with maintenance, smoking status, and systemic health. Individual outcomes vary.

Is there an age limit for implant treatment?

Age itself isn't a contraindication. We've successfully treated patients in their 70s and 80s. What matters is overall health status, healing capacity, and bone quality. If you're medically fit for tooth extraction under local anaesthetic, you're likely suitable for implant placement, though assessment is needed to confirm.

Are sedation options available?

Yes. For patients with anxiety about surgical procedures, we can arrange sedation. Options range from oral sedation to intravenous sedation, depending on your medical history and preferences. This is discussed during your assessment.
